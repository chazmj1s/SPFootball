# NCAA Power Ratings — User Guide

## Overview

NCAA Power Ratings is a college football analytics app that goes beyond win-loss records
to show you how teams are actually performing. Every ranking and projection is driven by
a statistical model that measures each team's performance against historical expectations,
adjusted for strength of schedule, home field advantage, and rivalry history.

---

## Navigation

The app is organized into five tabs at the top of the screen:

**Scores · Rankings · Following · Projections · Settings**

Use the year selector and week slider at the top to move through the season. All tabs
respond to the selected year and week.

---

## Scores

Your game-by-game view for the selected week. Shows actual scores for completed games
and projected scores for upcoming games.

Projected scores appear in parentheses `( )` so you can tell at a glance what has been
played and what is still ahead. Projections are generated by the same model that drives
the power ratings — they factor in each team's scoring average, points allowed, strength
of schedule, and the historical expected margin for that specific matchup.

---

## Rankings

The heart of the app. Shows all FBS teams ranked by the power rating model for the
selected week.

**What you see per team:**
- Overall rank among all FBS teams
- Record through the selected week
- Power Rating — the composite performance score
- Strength of Schedule (SOS)
- Conference and tier (P4, G5, Independent)

**Filtering:** Use the conference dropdown to narrow the list to a specific conference.
Use the filter button for quick views like Top 25, P4, or G5.

**Sorting:** Tap any column header to sort by that metric. Tap again to reverse the order.

**Offense / Defense panel:** Tap the "Offense / Defense" link below any team row to
expand a detailed breakdown showing:

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| Offensive Rank | Avg. Points Scored | Offensive Index (Std-Z) |
| Defensive Rank | Avg. Points Allowed | Defensive Index (Std-Z) |

The **Offensive and Defensive Index** values tell you how far above or below expectation
each unit performed, given who they played and where. A positive number means they
outperformed what the model expected. A negative number means they underperformed.
This is the "weren't they supposed to do better than this?" metric — it gives context
that raw points scored and allowed cannot.

**Why this matters:** A team can rank highly in average points scored simply by playing
weak opponents. The index adjusts for that. Similarly, a defense that holds a
high-powered offense to 17 points looks very different than one that holds a struggling
offense to 17 points. The model knows the difference.

---

## Following

Your personal view of the teams and games you care about most.

Follow a team by tapping the heart icon next to their name anywhere in the app. Followed
teams are pinned to the top of this tab so you never have to scroll to find them.

You can also follow specific games — look for the follow indicator on game cards in the
Scores tab. Followed games appear at the top of the Following tab above even the most
storied rivalry matchups.

---

## Projections

Forward-looking analysis for the rest of the season.

**Standings** — shows projected final conference standings based on predicted outcomes
for all remaining games. Updates automatically as results come in and the model
recalibrates.

**Title Game** — shows the projected conference championship game qualifiers for every
FBS conference. The model projects which two teams are most likely to meet in each
conference title game based on current standings and remaining schedules.

Projected results are clearly marked so you always know what is actual and what is
modeled.

---

## Settings

Customize how the app works for you.

**Default Conference** — set your home conference so the Rankings tab opens filtered
to your teams automatically. If you're an SEC fan you shouldn't have to filter every
time you open the app.

Additional settings for navigation behavior, display preferences, and notification
options will be added here as the app grows.

## Understanding the Rankings

### Power Rating
The core metric. Measures how a team performs relative to what was historically expected
given their opponents' strength, adjusted for home field and rivalry factors. A team
that consistently wins by more than expected against tough opponents will have a high
power rating. A team that wins close games against weak opponents will not.

### Strength of Schedule (SOS)
How hard has this team's schedule actually been? The model measures this two levels
deep — not just how good your opponents are, but how hard your opponents' opponents
were. A team in a weak conference that beats up on easy schedules will have a low SOS
regardless of their record.

### Offensive / Defensive Index
Context-adjusted performance for each side of the ball. Positive means better than
expected. Negative means worse than expected. Zero means exactly what the model
predicted. These numbers are on the same scale as the Power Rating so they're directly
comparable.

### Why rankings change week to week
Even in a bye week, a team's ranking can shift slightly as their opponents win or lose
games. A team you beat in week 4 going on a four-game winning streak improves your SOS
retroactively. This is by design — the model is always reflecting the most accurate
picture of schedule quality available.

### Early season rankings
Treat weeks 1 through 6 as directional rather than definitive. With only a handful of
games played, the model has limited data to work with and rankings can be volatile.
By week 7 the current season data is meaningful and the rankings stabilize considerably.

---

## A Note on Projections

All projected scores and standings are model outputs, not guarantees. College football
is gloriously unpredictable — that's why we watch. The model accounts for historical
upset rates and rivalry variance, but any given Saturday can produce results that defy
every reasonable expectation. That's the game.
